Rates vs. Prices

How the world's central banks used interest rates to fight the biggest inflation shock in four decades — and why different kinds of economies fought it differently.

Why interest rates?

When a central bank raises its policy rate, borrowing across the whole economy gets more expensive: mortgages, business loans, credit cards. Demand cools, hiring slows, the currency tends to strengthen — and with less money chasing goods, price growth eases. That chain of events is the monetary transmission mechanism, and it is the reason a single overnight interest rate is the main lever against inflation.

The real-rate rule

What matters is the real rate — the policy rate minus inflation. To slow an economy, a central bank must raise its rate by more than inflation rose (the “Taylor principle”), otherwise the real cost of borrowing is still falling. In 2021–22, with inflation near 10% and rates near zero, real rates were deeply negative almost everywhere — fuel on the fire that had to be withdrawn.

The long lag

Rate moves act with “long and variable lags” — typically 12–24 months to reach prices. Central banks therefore respond to where they think inflation is going, not where it is. In the charts below you can see the lag in both directions: hikes trail the inflation take-off, and disinflation trails the hikes.

Credibility & anchoring

Most central banks target ~2% (advanced) or 2–4% (emerging) inflation. If people believe the target, expectations stay “anchored” and inflation falls at a lower cost. Emerging markets, with a shorter credibility track record and fragile currencies, must usually hike sooner and higher for the same result — one of the clearest systemic differences in the data.

Data & method

  • Policy rates — BIS “Central bank policy rates” (WS_CBPOL), monthly, end of period, through . The BIS series is the reference cross-country source: it tracks each bank's headline instrument (fed funds target, ECB key rate, Selic, repo rate, 1-year LPR, …).
  • Inflation — headline consumer prices, year on year. Monthly figures 1980–2022 from the World Bank Global Database of Inflation (Ha, Kose & Ohnsorge); 2023–24 at annual resolution from the BIS long consumer-price series (drawn dashed); euro-area monthly HICP through 2025-04 from Eurostat. Australia reports CPI quarterly.
  • Latest-rate annotations for the Fed, ECB, Bank of England, Bank of Japan and SNB come from the BIS bulk download of 2026-05-06.
  • Groups follow the monetary regime, not geography: advanced majors, small open advanced economies, emerging-market inflation targeters, high-inflation outliers, and pegged/managed regimes.
  • Caveats — policy-rate definitions change over time within countries (footnotes in the BIS source); lag correlations are descriptive, not causal; annual inflation points are yearly averages and smooth over intra-year swings.
